Section 01 of 08
Your Story — In Your Own Words
Not the press bio. The real version — what actually happened, what you didn't know, what changed. This is what makes the content feel like a person wrote it.
Before you became "That Money Guy" — what was your actual relationship with money like? Growing up, at university, early career.
Not what you wish you'd done. What you actually did. Were you good with money? Bad? Completely clueless?
What's the money mistake you're most embarrassed about?
The kind you'd tell a close friend over dinner, not necessarily something you'd lead a video with.
When did you have the moment that made you think "I need to teach this stuff"? What actually happened?
As specific as you can — what was the conversation, the moment, the realisation?
What did you know nothing about financially when you left Barclays?
Things you had to figure out yourself after leaving a "real job" — the gaps that surprised you.
Complete this sentence in your own words:
"I used to think ______ about money. Now I know ______."
Section 02 of 08
Your Voice — Specific Language
Voice is the single most important thing to get right. Generic is the enemy. I need your actual words, your actual phrases, the things that are distinctly you.
Write 3 phrases or sentences you actually say all the time in your videos.
Transitions, intros, sign-offs, ways you explain things — things that are distinctly Gabriel.
Write 3 phrases or words you'd NEVER use — things that make you cringe in other finance content.
Jargon, buzzwords, phrases that feel fake, corporate, or condescending.
Finish this in your own voice: "Here's what most people get completely wrong about ISAs..."
Write it as if you're speaking to camera. Show me how you'd actually explain it.
Rewrite this sentence in your voice:
"It is advisable to consider diversifying your investment portfolio across multiple asset classes to mitigate risk."
Give me a piece of financial advice that sounds smart but is actually bad or misleading. Then give me your debunk.
Something you hear all the time that you disagree with.
How do you feel about using humour in finance content? What's your instinct on where the line is?
Section 03 of 08
Your Opinions — The Unfiltered Takes
This is what makes your content sound like YOU and not a Wikipedia article. I need to know what you actually think — not the "balanced" answer, the Gabriel answer.
Property vs. renting — what do you actually think?
This is one of the most debated topics in UK personal finance. Where do you genuinely land?
Crypto — where do you genuinely stand?
Fan, sceptic, somewhere in the middle? What's your actual position and why?
Index funds vs. picking individual stocks for someone just starting out?
The FIRE movement (Financial Independence, Retire Early) — fan or sceptic?
One financial "rule" or piece of advice that everyone repeats — and you think is overrated or oversimplified.
e.g. "always max your ISA", "6 months emergency fund", "pay off all debt before investing"
One financial topic almost nobody talks about that you think is genuinely important.
What's your take on the "loud luxury" / lifestyle inflation culture you see on social media?
Any other strong opinions in personal finance you want me to know? Things where you have a clear view that shapes how you create content?
Section 04 of 08
Your Audience — What You Actually Know
Not demographics. The real stuff — what you know about them from thousands of comments, DMs, and conversations.
What's the single most common misconception you see in your comments?
The thing that makes you think "I need to make another video about this because people still don't get it."
What question do people ask you most that you find surprisingly hard to answer well?
What does your comments section or DMs look like after a really good video? What are people actually saying?
Give me specific types of comments or DMs — not just "positive feedback".
What content do you make that you KNOW is right for your audience but consistently gets low views?
Describe your ideal audience member as if they were a specific person you actually know or have met.
Name them if you want. What's their job, their situation, what are they worried about, what do they want?
Is there anything your audience consistently gets wrong about you — a misperception you want to correct?
Section 05 of 08
Content Rules & Red Lines
What you won't do is just as important as what you will. This protects your voice and your audience — and makes sure I never accidentally suggest something that crosses a line for you.
A topic in personal finance you won't cover — and why.
Could be anything — a type of product, a type of content, a specific debate you won't touch.
A brand or product category you'd turn down regardless of the money.
Have you ever been asked to say or do something in a brand deal or collaboration that you refused? What was it?
As specific as you're comfortable being — this helps me understand your instincts.
A content format or style you genuinely dislike making — even if it performs well.
How do you think about the line between "educating your audience about a product" and "promoting something that might not be in their best interest"?
This is important for me to understand when I write branded content or CTAs.
Are there any topics where you deliberately stay neutral — where you have a personal view but choose not to share it publicly?
Section 06 of 08
Personality & Humour
The stuff between the educational content that makes people actually like you — not just learn from you.
Tell me a specific joke, line, or moment from your content that you were particularly proud of or that landed really well.
What's a specific analogy, comparison, or metaphor you use regularly to explain financial concepts?
The thing you always go back to because it works every time.
How would your best friend describe the way you explain money stuff — at a dinner party, not on camera?
What's something about you personally — not finance-related — that your most loyal audience members would know?
The stuff that makes you a person, not just a finance guy.
What type of humour do you actually find funny? (Give examples — creators, shows, comedians, specific things.)
What do you find annoying or cringeworthy in other creators' personalities — things you make a conscious effort not to do?
Section 07 of 08
What Good Looks Like
Your standard. The content you're proud of is the benchmark I use to calibrate everything else.
Name 3 pieces of your own content you're most proud of. For each: what was it, and why does it work?
Could be videos, posts, newsletter issues, anything. Links help if you have them.
Name a piece of content you made that you thought was great but that flopped. Why do you think it didn't land?
Describe the best comment or DM you've ever received from a viewer. What did it say?
The one that made you think "this is exactly why I do this."
Who outside your niche makes content you genuinely admire — on any platform, any topic? What do you admire about it?
Creators who make you think "I want to be this good at what they do."
If you had to describe your content at its absolute best in one sentence, what would you say?
Section 08 of 08
Where You're Going
The last section. This shapes the long-term direction — what I build towards, not just what exists now.
What topics do you want to start covering that you haven't touched yet — or haven't gone deep enough on?
What content would you make if the algorithm didn't exist? No views, no performance pressure — just what you'd want to say.
In 3 years, what do you want "That Money Guy" to mean to people?
Not follower counts. What do you want them to think or feel when they hear your name?
What do you want people to actually do differently in their financial lives because of your content?
The real-world outcomes you're working towards.
Tell me about the book. What is it, who's it for, what do you want it to do?
Anything else you want your AI head of content to know — about you, your brand, or what you're building?
Anything that didn't come up in the other sections. The things you'd want a new team member to understand about working with you.
